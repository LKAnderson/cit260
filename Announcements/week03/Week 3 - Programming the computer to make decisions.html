Ok, printing stuff out and doing some math was cool and all, but now we're finally getting to where the computer proves it's more than just an expensive calculator. We're going to program it to make some decisions for us.

In programming language terms, decision making is known as "selection." The code is selecting different blocks of code to run based on certain conditions you ask it to test. At the heart of all logic in computers (and in electrical engineering) is the boolean value, which can either be 1 or 0, or on or off, and in Java, it's true or false. 

It's funny to think that with all the complexities of computers and the things they are able to do for us in our day and age, the only real decision making ability they have is the ability to tell whether a value is true or false. But, oh, how we make use of that simple principle in some fantastic ways!

Tips

Here are some tips that might make your study a little more efficient and effective this week.

Tip #1 - Modulo (remainder) arithmetic

When we divide numbers that are of type double, we get a result that is a double. The nice thing about double (and float) is that they hold fractions. So 9.0 / 5 = 1.8 (or 1 and 4/5).

But with integers, we only get whole numbers. So 9 / 5 = 1, and we lose the rest of the number. Not cool. (Ok, it can be cool, but you need to know what you're doing with it.)

All programming languages support the modulo operator to provide the ability to get the remainder when doing integer division. So, as 9 / 5 = 1, 9 modulo 5 = 4 (the remainder).

In Java, the modulo operation is represented as a %. 9 % 5 = 4. (Don't confuse this with the % sign used inside strings when producing formatted output.)

Modulo division is extremely useful in many programming situations. We're going to get a feel for it this week in our assignments.

Tip #2 - Order of precedence in mathematical and logical expressions

Section 3.15 in the chapter is super important. There are many subtle bugs that arise because programmers forget about order of precedence in their expressions. I highly recommend you get familiar with this section. Of course, you can force certain calculations to happen first if you'll wrap them in parentheses. That helps the computer know what you mean with all those terms.
